# Clinical Evidence Stage Requirements

Use this reference when judging whether clinical evidence is sufficient for the asset's current phase and next decision.

## Phase 1 / Phase 1b

Expected evidence:

- Protocol, amendments, SAP if efficacy or biomarker claims are made.
- CSR or CSR synopsis for completed studies.
- Dose escalation rules, DLT review, stopping rules, and dose selection rationale.
- Subject disposition, demographics, baseline balance, exposure, and discontinuations.
- AE, SAE, AESI, deaths, lab, ECG, vital sign, and narrative summaries.
- PK, PD, target engagement, biomarker, and preliminary activity data if claimed.

Typical diligence conclusion:

- `Supportive with conditions` if safety, exposure, and early activity are coherent but still preliminary.
- `Not assessable` if only investor slides or selected charts are available.

## Phase 2

Expected evidence:

- Full CSR, protocol, amendments, SAP, tables, figures, listings, and datasets when available.
- Primary and secondary endpoint results with analysis population definitions.
- Comparator relevance and standard-of-care context.
- Safety database summary by dose, exposure, duration, discontinuation, and AESI.
- Subgroup, durability, missing data, sensitivity, and rescue-medication analyses.
- Draft Phase 3 rationale if the deal decision depends on registrational readiness.

Typical diligence conclusion:

- `Supportive` only when endpoint relevance, effect size, safety, and robustness are traceable.
- `Mixed` when signal is promising but primary documents or key analyses are missing.

## Phase 3 / Registration

Expected evidence:

- Full pivotal CSRs, protocols, SAPs, integrated efficacy and safety summaries.
- Prespecified endpoint hierarchy, multiplicity control, and sensitivity analyses.
- Subgroup consistency, durability, adherence, rescue therapy, and discontinuation analysis.
- Safety database adequacy for target indication and expected label.
- Label-relevant benefit-risk narrative.
- Regional applicability if licensing rights are geography-specific.

Typical diligence conclusion:

- `Supportive` when pivotal evidence is clinically meaningful, statistically robust, and label-relevant.
- `Not supportive` if benefit-risk is unfavorable or pivotal evidence fails key endpoints.

## Approved / Commercial

Expected evidence:

- Approval history, label, post-marketing safety, commitments, and real-world use.
- Comparative effectiveness or real-world evidence if commercial claims rely on it.
- Pharmacovigilance summaries, risk management plan, recalls, and major safety updates.

Typical diligence conclusion:

- Focus shifts from "does it work?" to label strength, durability of use, safety over time, and commercial evidence.
